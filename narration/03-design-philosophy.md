Chapter three. Design philosophy.

We've seen the shape of the platform. Now let's talk about the handful of ideas that hold that shape together — because everything you'll meet later is one of these three ideas, worn in a different costume.

Here's the thing to understand first. None of these principles were drawn on a whiteboard before the code. They emerged from one question, asked again and again, every time something new came up: does this belong to the platform, or to the resolver? And every time the honest answer was "the resolver," the platform got a little smaller, and a little harder to break.

So the first idea is mechanism, not policy. The platform provides the machinery. It gives you a lifecycle — create, track, pause, resume, cancel. It gives you isolation, a sealed container for each run. It gives you transport, the way events move and stream. And it checks that the shapes are right. But notice what it never does. It never decides what inputs a request needs, or what they mean. It never decides how many phases the work has, or what counts as "done," or "verified," or "good." All of that is policy, and policy lives in the resolver. There's a bright line here, and I want you to hold onto it: the platform validates structure; it never interprets meaning. The moment you let the platform start understanding what a "spec" is, or whether a repository makes sense for a task, every new resolver becomes a change to the platform itself. Hold the line, and the platform stays a constant while resolvers become a free variable.

The second idea follows from the first. The center stays still. Someone ships a brand-new resolver — writes a package, registers it, points a request at it — and the daemon does not move. Not one line. New phases, new kinds of output, new ways of interacting, new ideas about verification: all of it handled out at the edges. This is why three resolvers as different as a compiled pipeline, a live delegation manager, and a contract-governed apprentice can all run on identical platform code. The whole architecture fits in one sentence. Resolvers depend on the platform. The platform depends on no resolver.

The third idea is ruthless isolation. And this one isn't abstract — it was paid for. There was a real incident: more than four thousand runaway test processes, a hundred gigabytes of memory consumed, the machine killing things to survive. The answer was three independent layers of defense, none of them trusted to be enough on its own. A kernel limit, a process guardian, a watchdog — each catching a different kind of failure. Because autonomous agents will do surprising things, and isolation is what makes "surprising" something you survive instead of something that takes you down.

There's a fourth idea that ties to observability, and it deserves a moment, because it sets up one of the most important rules in this whole masterclass. A run you cannot see is a run you cannot trust, or resume, or debug. But the platform isn't allowed to look inside the resolver's reasoning — that would cross the bright line. So the resolution is a small, fixed vocabulary of events that every resolver agrees to emit. And the keystone of that vocabulary is completion. A run is complete when it says it is complete — by emitting a typed, explicit event — with the process exit as a backup. Never by printing a magic word into a log and hoping someone greps for it. We'll return to this. For now, just file it away: the resolver must announce its own ending.

So here's the whole game, in one line. Keep the mechanism old, so the strategies can be new. Everything that follows is that sentence, made concrete, one subsystem at a time.
