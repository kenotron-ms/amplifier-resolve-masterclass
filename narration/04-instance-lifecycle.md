Chapter four. The instance lifecycle.

If the instance is the unit of work, then the lifecycle is the spine of the platform. Everything the daemon does is in service of moving one instance from birth to rest and telling you, at every moment, exactly where it is. Master this, and you've got the backbone of the whole system.

Now, this chapter has a diagram, and if you're listening, you can't see it — so let me draw it for you in the air, because its shape is the lesson.

Picture the page laid out left to right, like reading a sentence. On the far left sits a box called "created." A short arrow carries you to the next box, "starting." Another arrow, and you arrive at the big one in the middle: "running." That straight line — created, starting, running — is the happy path. It's how every instance begins, and you read it just like a sentence, left to right.

Now, from "running," the picture opens up. Look to the right edge, and you'll find three boxes stacked in a column — these are the endings, the terminal states. The top one is "completed," the finish for a run that succeeded. Below it, "failed," for a run that hit an error. And below that, "cancelled," for a run someone stopped on purpose. Arrows fan out from "running" toward these three. Once you land in any of them, you stop. There's no arrow leaving. That's what "terminal" means.

But there's a subtler part of the picture, and it's the most important. Above and around "running" sit two more boxes — "awaiting input" and "paused" — and the arrows that connect them to "running" point both ways. That's the detail to hold onto: they're round trips, not exits. The run reaches "awaiting input" when the resolver stops to ask a human a question, and an arrow labeled "response" carries it right back to "running" once the answer arrives. The run reaches "paused" when it suspends itself, and an arrow labeled "resume" brings it back. So the shape, all together, is this: a straight line in, three doors out on the right, and two side-rooms it can step into and come back from.

And here's what the picture is really teaching. Those two side-loops — awaiting input and paused — are exactly what separate a governed run from a plain script. A script doesn't suspend itself for an hour waiting on a person and then carry on where it left off. An instance does. That's why an instance is not a session. Those two states are the whole difference, drawn in the geometry. Now — one careful note. The platform owns those states as vocabulary; it can report them and act on them, route a response, resume on request. But it never decides when a resolver should pause or block. The status is mechanism; the decision to enter it is the resolver's policy.

Underneath those eight statuses, every instance moves through four phases. First, intake: a request arrives, the platform validates the input against the resolver's rules — and if it's malformed, it's rejected right there, before any container exists. Second, provision: the platform builds the sealed container, writes in the configuration, clones the repositories, and launches the worker. Third, execute: the worker runs the resolver, and this is where the strategy plays out — opaque to the platform by design. The platform sees the stream of events, never the reasoning. And fourth, finalize: the platform recognizes the run is over, moves it to its terminal state, and tears the container down. Cleanup is not optional; containers are not allowed to leak, even after a crash.

One last piece — how does the platform know a run is over? It watches from outside, polling the files the worker writes. And it reconciles completion in strict priority. First, the completion event, if the resolver emitted one — authoritative, carrying the outcome and any structured error. Second, the process exit code, as a fallback. And third, last of all, a watchdog timeout. Event first, exit second. That order is the platform-side mirror of the resolver's own promise — announce your ending at every exit path — which is exactly where we go next.
