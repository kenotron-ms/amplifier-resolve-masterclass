Chapter eight. A-2-U-I and the interaction surfaces.

Events let you watch a run. But autonomous work isn't fire-and-forget. Sometimes the resolver needs to ask you something. Sometimes you need to reach in and steer. So the question for this chapter is a delicate one: how do you let a resolver define rich, dynamic interactions — forms, tables, decisions, approval gates — without the platform understanding any of them? The answer is a wire format called A-2-U-I.

Here's the idea. A resolver describes an interaction — a form to fill, a table to review, a decision to make — as a tree of components in this A-2-U-I format. A human-facing consumer renders that tree as a native form on screen. An agent consumer reads it programmatically. And the platform's only job, in the middle, is to check that an incoming answer satisfies the rules the resolver declared — the required fields are present, the checks pass. It never interprets what the form means. It doesn't know that one field is a repository address and another is an approval. This is the bright line again, now wearing the costume of a protocol: the resolver owns meaning, the platform owns structure. And it's exactly what lets a resolver invent a brand-new kind of interaction — a multi-panel review gate, a decision override — without the platform changing a line.

Now, there are exactly three ways a resolver and a consumer exchange structured information, and the cleanest way to keep them straight is to ask two questions of each: when does it happen, and who starts it?

The first surface is instantiation. This happens before the instance even exists, and the consumer starts it. You're setting up a run, so you ask the resolver what it needs, and it hands back a form. There's a nice subtlety here called progressive discovery — the form can change based on what you've already filled in. Pick a pipeline, and the form reveals that pipeline's specific fields. The completed form becomes the input to creating the instance, validated before any container is ever built.

The second surface is input requests. This happens during the run, and the resolver starts it. Mid-flight, the resolver decides it needs an answer to continue — so it asks. The platform writes out the question, emits the event, and the instance typically moves to "awaiting input," that side-room from the lifecycle chapter. You fetch the pending question, submit your answer, the platform validates it and hands it to the resolver, and the run carries on.

The third surface is messages. This can happen anytime after creation, and the consumer starts it — unsolicited. This is steering. And there's a lovely detail: what you're allowed to say depends on where the run is. The consumer asks for the message catalog for the current state, and the resolver answers based on that state. So the vocabulary of steering shifts as the run moves. You post your message, the platform validates it, and the resolver receives it.

Three surfaces. Instantiation, consumer-first and before the run. Input requests, resolver-first and during the run. Messages, consumer-first and anytime. Why keep them so cleanly separated? Because collapsing them would blur the one thing the platform most needs to track — who is waiting on whom. That's exactly what lets the platform model "awaiting input" correctly. Three clean surfaces keep the routing unambiguous.

I want to show you one piece of craftsmanship underneath all this, because it's quietly beautiful. When a resolver asks a question, the call returns a future — a promise of an answer not yet arrived. The resolver can wait on it, blocking until the human responds. Or it can hold it and handle the answer later. Or it can ignore it and keep working. This is what makes human-in-the-loop composable: a gate isn't a special control-flow mode bolted onto the system. A gate is just an await on a future that the platform completes when your answer lands. Whether to wait, continue, or time out stays the resolver's policy. The platform only provides the mechanism that completes the future.

And one last channel, for the things too big for a form. Large or arbitrary artifacts — a rendered graph, a diff, a generated file — don't travel through the event log. They ride a separate, equally resolver-agnostic channel: the data bus. The resolver writes a file, emits a tiny pointer saying "this path changed," and the consumer fetches exactly that path, exactly when it changes. Never pushed, never polled — fetched on demand. The platform serves the bytes and never looks inside them. It's the whole philosophy in miniature: the platform moves data it does not interpret.
