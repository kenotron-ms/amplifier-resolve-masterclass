Chapter ten. Three resolvers, three theories of correctness.

This is where the whole architecture pays off. Three resolvers ship today. They satisfy the same protocol, emit the same vocabulary, and run on the same engine. The platform cannot tell them apart. And yet each one answers a different, almost philosophical question: how should an AI agent be governed, so that you can trust what it produces? Three answers, and they could not be more opposed. Studying them side by side is the fastest way to feel the design space — and to understand why the platform refuses to take a side.

The first is dot-graph, and it treats resolution as a compiled artifact. Picture the strategy authored ahead of time, as a graph: every node in that graph becomes its own independent agent session, and the resolver itself is just a thin loader — the intelligence lives in the drawing, not the code. The theory of correctness here is: decide the whole strategy in advance, then execute it deterministically. Fan-out, fan-in, consensus across different models, human gates — all of it expressed as the shape of the graph, not as runtime decisions. Because the strategy is a file, you can review it, diff it, and reuse it before anything ever runs. It trades runtime flexibility for reproducibility. The right choice when you want the same resolution to happen the same way, every single time.

The second is the orchestrator, and it treats resolution as live delegation. Picture a manager who never touches the code. One long-lived session sits at the center, and it doesn't execute — it plans, delegates, monitors, and synthesizes. It's handed a task in plain language and a small suite of tools: launch a worker, check a worker, message a worker, destroy a worker, ask the human. It figures out how to break the work apart as it goes. Workers are autonomous sessions in their own isolated containers, running in parallel, collected and torn down when they finish. The theory of correctness is: don't commit to a plan up front — decompose at runtime and apply judgment, and when you're unsure, ask. Maximum flexibility, minimum guarantees. The exact inverse of dot-graph. The right choice when the work is open-ended and you can't know its shape before you start.

The third is the understudy, and it's the most opinionated of all: trust nothing unverified. Picture a disciplined apprentice who agrees on the goal before acting, and proves the result after. It moves through four phases, bracketed by two human approvals. First it aligns — reads the codebase, drafts a vision of what success means, and shows you that vision for sign-off. Then, before doing any work, it commits to a handful of verifiable journeys — concrete promises, each with a stated way to check it. Then it executes. Then — and this is the heart of it — an independent verifier, a separate session that never saw the worker's reasoning, checks each promise one by one. And the results are honest: pass, fail, or genuinely unknown. The design refuses to overstate its own confidence. A final gate approves the result against the original intent. Correctness here is contractual and proven, never assumed. It's the most expensive strategy, and the right one when a confident wrong answer would cost you dearly.

So: three resolvers. Same contract, same events, same engine — and three completely different theories of what makes an answer trustworthy. One puts correctness in the design of a graph. One puts it in runtime judgment. One puts it in independent verification. And here is the line that makes the whole thing sing: the platform endorses none of them. If it had an opinion about how resolution should work, two of these three could not exist. Because it only checks structure and routes events, all three live side by side — and a fourth, one nobody has imagined yet, can join them without changing the platform at all. These three resolvers aren't just features. They're the proof that the bright line holds.
