Chapter twelve. Provisioning and the inner loop.

Everything so far has described the platform running. This chapter is about changing it — the loop you work in to make a change and prove it actually works. It's the part new maintainers most often get wrong, because the topology is subtle and the failure modes are silent. Master it, and you can prove a change is real. Miss it, and your tests pass while the platform quietly breaks.

It centers on something called the Digital Twin Universe — a faithful twin of production in a box. It's a single container that stands up the whole stack: backend, resolvers, viewports. You develop against it as if it were the real thing.

Now let me describe the topology diagram, because its shape is the whole lesson, and if you're listening you can't see it. Picture a wide box across the middle of the page labeled "the bridge" — think of it as a shared network street. Sitting on that street is the Digital Twin Universe itself, the box holding the resolve stack. And here's the crucial part: the workers, and the reality-check container that verifies behavior, are drawn as separate boxes also sitting on that same street — each connected to the bridge by an arrow labeled "sibling." They are not nested inside the Digital Twin Universe. They sit beside it, as peers, on the same network. Now look downward from the Digital Twin Universe: there's one more box hanging below it, the Gitea source server, and its arrow is labeled differently — "hosts," and it lives inside the universe, as a container within the container.

So hold those two relationships apart, because they're the source of nearly every pitfall here. The workers and the reality-check are siblings — outside the unit, next to it. The Gitea server is inside it. Most provisioning bugs come from confusing those two facts.

Watch how it plays out. When you want to test local changes, the harness mirrors your working tree — uncommitted files and all — into that inner Gitea server. But here's the trap: that mirror is a one-time snapshot, taken once, at launch. Edit a file afterward — even by an agent's own hand, mid-session — and that edit never reaches Gitea. Workers spawned later still install the launch-time state. So the discipline is simple and unforgiving: change, then re-launch. Never assume a live edit reached the workers.

And addressing. Because the workers are siblings — out on the street, not inside the unit — they cannot reach Gitea by saying "localhost" or "the host gateway." Both of those point at the wrong machine. The only address that works is the unit's own address on that shared bridge. Send a worker to the host gateway and its clone fails — but it fails only inside the twin, while passing perfectly on your own laptop. That's the worst kind of bug, and it comes straight from the sibling-versus-inside distinction.

The same theme repeats. Mirroring your code does nothing unless the resolver's manifest explicitly opts in; forget that one line, and the run silently uses the upstream version, and your validation proves nothing. And a worker's file mounts must point at host paths, not paths inside the unit — because the workers are the host's containers, siblings, and the only filesystem they share with the twin is the host's. "The unit can see the file" does not mean "the worker can see the file."

Which brings us to the recurring lesson of the whole chapter: unit tests pass while integration boundaries break. Every pitfall here is invisible to a unit test and visible only in a real run of the twin. That's exactly why the merge gate lives where it does. Heavier verification for heavier risk — and for the riskiest changes, a throwaway twin is launched, run end to end, and torn down. "Tests are green" answers "did I break the pieces?" It does not answer "does the change do the thing?" The gate is placed precisely at the boundary where the expensive bugs live.

Understand the inner loop, and you can finally trust your own changes. Next, we assemble the whole platform and look at where it's going.
