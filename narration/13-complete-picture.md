Chapter thirteen. The complete picture, and where it's going.

Every box from the architecture map back in chapter two is now familiar to you. So in this chapter we assemble them into one layered model — and then we look, honestly, at the open edges. Because mastery includes knowing what is not finished.

Let me describe the layer diagram, because its shape is the whole summary, and if you're listening you can't see it. Picture six horizontal bands, stacked one on top of another, with an arrow running down through all of them. We read it from the bottom up, and the rule is simple: each layer rests only on the ones below it, and each can be replaced without disturbing what it sits on.

At the very bottom is layer one, the runtime — the containers and the filesystem bus, the raw isolation that everything else stands on. Resting on it, layer two, the platform: the daemon itself. Hold onto that one — it's the still center, and we'll come back to it. On top of the platform sits layer three, the SDK, the shared session engine from chapter nine. On the SDK rest the resolvers, layer four — the three strategies. Above them, layer five, the consumer layer: the app shell and its dynamic viewports. And crowning the stack, layer six, the edge — the browsers, the agents, the command line, the continuous-integration bridges, all the ways work actually arrives.

Now here's the lesson the picture teaches. Look again at layer two, the platform, sitting low in the stack but central to everything. The layers below it are how work is isolated; the layers above it are how work gets done and shown. The platform depends on none of them — and it offers a contract to all of them. There are exactly four such contracts: the resolver protocol, the event vocabulary, the interaction format, and the viewport props. Four contracts hold all six layers together. That's the entire architecture in a single glance.

And the payoff of building it this way is that "where does my new thing go?" almost always has a boring answer. Want a new resolution strategy? Add a resolver — the platform, the engine, and the shell stay untouched. A new git host? Add a provider. A new container runtime? Replace the bottom layer; everything above it is fine. A new way to trigger runs? Add an edge integration that posts a request. The rule of thumb: if your change forces you to edit the platform daemon itself, stop and ask whether what you're adding is really mechanism — or whether it's policy that belongs in a resolver, a provider, or an edge. Almost always, it's the latter.

Now, honesty about the edges. The flagship integration — the GitHub loop of triage, then implement, then review — is the first consumer being built out end to end. It is not the platform's purpose; it's one event-driven consumer among an open-ended set, and the platform stays consumer-agnostic beneath it. There's active work to finish that chain. And there are known open edges held deliberately in view: nested pipelines that aren't yet wired, a router that needs a cleaner way to express conditional branches, the cross-session stitching that's landed on one side and still pending on the other.

And there's one decision worth admiring, because it shows the discipline. The event vocabulary has no version field at all. None. Why? Because today every consumer lives in-tree, so the team can change the contract freely. But there's a named trigger written down: the moment a third-party resolver author depends on that contract, introduce versioning before merging the change that breaks it. The trigger exists so the decision can't be forgotten — not because the policy is already decided. Don't pay for generality until a second consumer makes it real, but write down exactly what will force your hand. That's ruthless simplicity applied to a contract.

So here is Resolve, in one closing line. It is a single idea pursued without compromise: keep the mechanism old, so the strategies can be new. The platform validates structure and routes events; it never interprets meaning. And from that one restraint, everything follows — the lifecycle, the sealed container, the vocabulary, the surfaces, the engine, the three opposing resolvers, the plugin faces, the inner loop. Every one of them is a different face of the same bright line. When you change the platform, your real job is to defend that line — to keep asking whether the thing you're adding is mechanism the platform should own, or policy that belongs somewhere else. Get that judgment right, consistently, and the center stays still while the edges move at any speed. That is what it means to be a master of Resolve.
